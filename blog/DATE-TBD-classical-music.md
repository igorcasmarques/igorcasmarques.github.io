## Is video game music Classical?

The other day, I watched a video by guitarist Adam Monica (AKA [TV on Guitar](https://tvonguitar.com/index)). Monica specializes in "Classical guitar covers" of music from popular media like TV shows and video games. I like this intersection.

<!-- truncate -->

In the video, Monica played an [arrangement of Vega's theme](https://youtu.be/H4KoRvkcTxU?si=zBrP8vsHoUnqrJtU) from *Street Fighter II* (1991). Both the arrangement and the performance are great. Monica's got a very crisp sense of rhythm, his counterpoint is clear and controlled, and his flamenco flourishes are flawless.

In response to Monica's impressive technical display, a user said,

</figure>

This is one of the video's top-rated comments. Lots of people think it's a good take. But since this is the Internet—where the more thumbs-up a comment receives, the more contrarians it attracts—this comment also made some people upset.

Some people got defensive. They didn't appreciate the implication that Classical music was inherently superior to video game music, and said things like,

>It IS a classical masterpiece. Just because it's from a video game doesn't invalidate it in any way.

It's a bold assertion. Vega's theme, a "Classical masterpiece"? Like, comparable to Tárrega's *Recuerdos de la Alhambra*? Or Albéniz's *Asturias*?

I don't know about that.

But, most importantly, why does this person think that Vega's theme would be "invalidated" if it were treated as something other than a "Classical masterpiece"? Where is this insecurity coming from?

The sentiment that video game music and Classical music belong together is not uncommon among gamers. I've seen variations of this argument pop up every now and again in online discourse about video game music.

Sergio Paul (AKA [Vaughan Sankofa](https://sankofaviolin.wixsite.com/sankofasound)), a musician and up-and-coming YouTuber, takes this argument as far as it can go. He put it this way,

>[Video game music] is a direct continuation and evolution of the Classical instrumental genre. As far as I'm concerned, these game composers *are* our Mozarts and Beethovens—maybe even more so than the film composers of our generation.
>
>[*Are Game OSTs the New Classical Music?*](https://youtu.be/Ur7MhinL5_A?si=l399fCY2BGJVQIqO) (0:30-0:41)

Another bold claim. It accepts, however implicitly, the pedestal we put Classical music on, then it insists we should revere video game music in a similar way. It proposes that the work of someone like [Yoko Shimomura](https://yokoshimomura.com/), the composer of *Street Fighter II*'s soundtrack, is on par, quality- and relevance-wise, with the big names of the Classical canon.

Again, this sounds like a bit of a stretch for me. But I understand why Paul and others like him feel this way: it's all about legitimization.

## Legitimization

Gamers have an intense connection with the games they play. It's hard for non-gamers to understand just how powerful this connection can be.

In a very real sense, video games shape who gamers are, how they see themselves and the world, what values they hold dear. Gaming can be a haven from life's troubles. It can be a reminder of simpler, happier times. Gaming can be as fulfilling and exhilarating to gamers as film, music, and literature are to other people.

Gaming makes gamers happy. Plain and simple.

So, when someone comes around and says that video games are not "art," or that they're a waste of time, a childish hobby, or anything in this spirit, gamers take it personally. And that's totally understandable. How dare people disparage the thing that's so valuable to them?

I can think of two main reasons why some people would think video game music *is* Classical music. One reason is logical, the other is emotional. Both have their merits.


First, the logical reason.

Historically speaking, writing video game music was an incredibly deliberate process. Think games, especially console games, from the 1980s and 1990s. Their music had to be programmed note by note, one minute detail at a time. Rhythm, timbre, volume, phrasing, counterpoint—every artistic decision was full of intention. The nature of the medium demanded this type of approach.

And then, once the work was done, the music became fixed. A sequence of commands, baked into the game's code, reiterated verbatim each playthrough.

Now consider this: doesn't this type of detail-driven approach to composition sound a lot like how we imagine Classical composers poring over the page, imbuing every note with purpose and meaning?

And the fixidity of game music, isn't it like how we have traditionally regarded Classical music scores as fixed and definitive?

But there's more. Game composers weren't free to express their creativity however they wanted. They had to negotiate with hardware constraints and the overarching concern for a positive player experience. From an intellectual standpoint, there's a lot to admire about video game music that managed to strike a good balance between the competing priorities of artistic effect and practicality.

But of course, much has changed in the decades since. As technology advanced, video game music became freer, more hi-fi, more malleable in its relationship with the interactive and dynamic elements of games. Video game soundtracks are now perfectly capable of featuring symphonic scores, chart-topping pop songs, and everything in between. And our expectations about audio production in games is as high as any other media, such as film or commercial music.

But the ethos of those early decades of video game music lingers on. A strong sense of intentionality and purposefulness remains. And with that, the link with Classical music.

### Emotional ties

The second reason has more to do with emotion.

There is a lot to admire about this video game music intellectually, but I honestly don't think this is the main reason people like Paul say video game music is modern Classical music.

It's common for gamers to feel an intense emotional connection with the video games they played. To many, the stories they lived through while gaming are as meaningful and touching as movies and literature may be for others. This is especially true for games they played when they were younger, during their formative years.

Sound and music are an integral part of the gaming experience.
